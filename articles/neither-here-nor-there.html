Neither Here Nor There

Or, The Permanent Art of Being the Wrong Kind of Person Everywhere

There is a very specific personality disorder that is not in the DSM but absolutely should be.

It is called: being an immigrant kid.

Or worse — being the child of immigrants. Because then you are not just living one life. You are running two operating systems at once and both of them keep asking for updates.

You are too foreign at home. Too local abroad. Too Western for the cousins. Too ethnic for the coworkers. Too much of one thing for one room. Not enough of it for the next.

You spend your whole life feeling like a software glitch in human form.

Congratulations. You are the bridge. Which sounds poetic until you realize bridges just get walked on.

This starts early. As a child, you realize very quickly that your house is not like other people's houses. At school, your lunch smells like culture. At home, your parents think Lunchables are a hate crime.

Other kids have names teachers can pronounce. You have a name substitute teachers approach like they're diffusing a bomb. They stare at attendance sheets like your existence is a pronunciation challenge.

"Is… uh… Say-ed? Syed? Sayed? Samuel?"

Sure. Let's go with Kevin.

You learn to laugh. Because if you don't laugh, you'll have to explain your entire family tree before first period.

Then there are the family parties. The Olympics of identity confusion. You walk in and immediately get hit with three things: food, interrogation, and an aunt you have never seen before asking why you've gotten so skinny and so fat at the same time.

Relatives speak to you in your mother tongue with absolute confidence. You reply like Google Translate during a power outage. Half a sentence. Three English words. A nervous smile. They look at you like your ancestors are filing formal complaints.

"Your language is getting weak."

Yes, because apparently I chose to be born in subtitles.

At home, they say: "Why are you acting so American?" Outside, people ask: "Where are you really from?"

Nowhere. Everywhere. Spiritually? The airport.

That question — "Where are you really from?" — is one of history's greatest passive-aggressive magic tricks. Because it sounds innocent. But what it often means is: you seem like you belong here, suspiciously. Please explain.

And if you answer with your actual hometown? "No, but originally?"

Brother, I was three years old. My original form was crying. What answer are we looking for?

Sometimes I want to say: I'm from the deep emotional damage section. Aisle seven. Near the generational trauma.

The funny part is, when you finally go "back home," you realize you're foreign there too. You arrive thinking: finally, my people. Immediately, they clock you. The accent. The clothes. The way you ask for Wi-Fi before water. You are exposed in minutes.

You thought you were returning to your roots. Turns out, your roots think you're weird.

Beautiful. Now you are internationally confused. You are not a person. You are a cultural exchange program.

Even language betrays you. You think in one language. Feel in another. Argue in English. Apologize in your mother tongue. Panic in both.

Some emotions don't even translate. There are words your parents use that take five English sentences and still somehow lose their emotional violence. Especially disappointment. Immigrant-parent disappointment is an art form.

No one says "I'm disappointed" like a dramatic American movie parent. No. They say: "Do whatever you want." And suddenly you feel like you have personally ruined civilization. That sentence has the emotional power of a court summons.

And let's talk about careers. Because immigrant households do not believe in "following your passion." Passion is for people with trust funds. Your options are: Doctor. Engineer. Lawyer. Or family disappointment. Maybe pharmacist if your aunt is feeling progressive.

You say, "I want to write." They hear: "I would like to be unemployed professionally."

You say, "I'm exploring." They hear: "I have declared war on stability."

You could cure loneliness itself and someone would still ask: "Yes, but is there dental insurance?"

Honestly? Fair. These people crossed oceans. You think they survived dictatorship, war, and paperwork for you to become a podcast host? Be serious.

And then there is romance. Diaspora dating deserves its own United Nations committee. You are trying to find love while balancing your own standards, your parents' standards, your religion's standards, your aunt's standards for some reason, and a cousin who keeps sending biodata like they work for LinkedIn Marriage Edition.

You bring someone home and suddenly everyone becomes an FBI profiler. "What does their father do?" I don't know, man, I just like how they laugh. Apparently that is not enough. Love is beautiful. But can love speak the language and own property? These are separate questions.

And beneath all the comedy, there is something heavier. Because living between worlds means constantly editing yourself. At work, you soften your name. At home, you sharpen your accent. With friends, you explain your parents. With parents, you explain your friends. You become a translator for everyone, including yourself.

And after enough years, you start wondering: which version is actually me? The one at home? The one outside? The one who code-switches so fast it deserves an Olympic medal? Or is identity just that — motion?

Maybe being "in-between" is not confusion. Maybe it is fluency. Maybe belonging to two worlds means you get to see both more clearly. Maybe being neither is secretly being both.

That took me a while to learn. Because for years, I thought home was a place I had to choose. This country or that one. This language or that one. This version of myself or the other.

But maybe home is not geography. Maybe home is recognition. The people who understand your joke before you finish it. The smell of food that fixes your mood. The song your parents played while cleaning on Sundays. The friend who also lies to their family by saying they are "five minutes away."

Home is where explanation becomes unnecessary.

And sometimes, weirdly, home is the tension itself. The in-between. The airport gate. The phone call. The half-translated sentence. The place where contradiction lives.

Because identity is not always a clean answer. Sometimes it is just learning to stop apologizing for being complicated.

You are allowed to belong in more than one place. You are allowed to be made of mixed things. Language. Memory. Migration. Loss. Humor. Shame. Pride. You are allowed to be unfinished. That is not failure. That is inheritance.

So yes, diaspora life is funny. It is aunties judging your life choices while handing you dessert. It is explaining to friends why "take food home" is not a suggestion but a legal requirement. It is being told to "act normal" by people who believe turmeric can solve Wi-Fi issues.

It is chaos. But it is also beautiful. Because there is something powerful about being proof that survival traveled. That your family crossed oceans and somehow you are here — bad at your native language, good at pretending you understand taxes, emotionally attached to both countries during the World Cup.

That matters. You are not lost. You are evidence.

And maybe that is enough.

So if someone asks where you are really from, maybe just smile. And say: depends. Emotionally or geographically?

Because geographically, I can explain. Emotionally? Honestly, I'm from a kitchen where nobody said "I love you," but everyone asked if I wanted more rice.

And if that doesn't make sense to them, that's okay. Some people need Google Maps. Some of us were raised by directions like: "Turn where the old bakery used to be."

And somehow, we still found our way home.
