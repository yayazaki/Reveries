I Don't Like Ariana Saeed and That Was Apparently a Problem

June 2026

I don't like Ariana Saeed. There. Said it. Not in a hateful way, not in a "I wish her ill" way. She has a voice, she has fans who love her deeply, and clearly she means something real to a lot of people. I get it. I just don't feel it. Never did. Some things I heard about her over the years didn't help. Could be rumors. Probably some of it is. But the impression stuck, and my impression is: not for me. That's it.

Except it wasn't the whole thing for my ex.

My ex was Afghan. Ariana Saeed is Afghan. And in the diaspora, that combination carries weight. She's not just a singer, she's a symbol, a source of pride, a "look what we produced" moment for a community that doesn't always get those. I understand that. I respect it. I just didn't connect with the music, and some of what I'd heard made it harder.

None of that was acceptable.

The campaign started early. Concert invitations. Specific songs sent with the note "just listen to this one, it's different." YouTube videos of live performances. The occasional "how can you not feel this?" delivered with genuine bafflement, like my not feeling it was a medical condition requiring treatment. And every time I gave my standard answer, "she's just not my thing," I could feel the room change.

"Why not though?"

"I just don't connect with it."

"But she's Afghan."

"I know."

"So?"

That "so" is worth sitting with, because it's doing a lot of work in one tiny word. It's not an aesthetic argument. It's a loyalty test. You are Afghan, she is Afghan, therefore your preference is not really a preference, it's a political position. Your "not for me" becomes "not proud enough" or "not connected enough," or evidence of something that needs to be fixed. The stakes inflate fast. A song becomes a referendum on who you are.

And that's when I started to understand what was actually going on. My ex didn't need me to enjoy the music. She needed me to convert. Because conversion meant something. It would mean her influence was strong enough to shift something I'd already decided. That she knew better. That I was, in some small but real way, changed because of her.

This is a specific kind of manipulation, and it's sneaky because it wears enthusiasm as a costume. It looks like sharing. It looks like passion. "I just want you to experience something I love" sounds generous, even selfless. But the tell is what happens when you keep saying no. If it was really about the music, a polite pass would be the end of the conversation. Instead, your refusal becomes the problem. Stubbornness. Proof that you don't value her judgment. And slowly, if you're not paying attention, you start auditing yourself. Maybe I haven't given it a real chance. Maybe I'm being difficult. Maybe three more listens with an open mind would settle it.

I gave it several more listens. It didn't settle anything. I just don't like the music.

And that should have been enough. Not liking something is not a wound that needs healing. It's not closed-mindedness dressed up as taste. It's a preference, and preferences don't require a three-step rehabilitation program. I didn't owe her a conversion. I didn't owe her the version of me that finally came around.

The people who can't let you say "no thanks" are not trying to share something beautiful with you. They're trying to confirm something about themselves. Your taste is the canvas. Their influence is what they're actually painting.

What they never tell you, and what took me too long to figure out, is that you were never the project. You were the proof.
